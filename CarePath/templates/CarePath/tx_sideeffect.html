Possible Treatments and Side effects to Expect



Most head and neck cancers are treated with surgery, radiotherapy, chemotherapy or a combination of these modalities.


Surgery

Surgery involves the removal of abnormal or diseased tissue from the head and neck. Some head and neck cancers are treated with surgery alone. Some are treated with surgery followed by radiation therapy (or a combination of radiation therapy and chemotherapy). If your surgery is scheduled to be done at Auckland City Hospital, you may be asked to attend a pre-admission clinic. This clinic is held in the afternoon of the same day. At this clinic you will see a nurse and an anaesthetist to help you prepare for surgery. You may require further tests. This clinic usually takes an additional 3-4 hours to complete. If your surgery is to be done at another hospital, an appointment for you to attend a pre-admission clinic will be arranged for a different day.


Radiotherapy (Radiation Therapy)

Radiotherapy is the use of high-energy radiation to destroy cancer cells or prevent them from reproducing. Radiotherapy only affects the part of the body that the radiation is aimed at. Some cancers are treated with radiotherapy alone. Some cancers are treated with radiotherapy and chemotherapy. Some cancers are treated with surgery followed by radiotherapy. Sometimes radiation can affect the teeth and mouth. Your teeth need to be healthy before you start radiotherapy. The MDM will organise a dental checkup and any urgent dental treatment needed. This will be done by the hospital dental service. There will be no cost for you.


Chemotherapy and other drug treatments

Chemotherapy, immunotherapy and targeted therapies are drug treatments aimed to disrupt the growth of cancer cells. They are usually given by an injection into a vein but sometimes by mouth. Chemotherapy is usually given with radiotherapy for head and neck cancer. It also may be given as a treatment on its own. Immunotherapy and targeted therapies are newer drug treatments emerging for treating specific types of head and neck cancer.



Possible Side effects

Patients undergoing radiotherapy can experience several unwanted oral side effects, which have both short-term and long-term implications.


Common short-term side effects include:

• Cellulitis - bacterial skin infection
• Mucositis - inflammation/ulceration of the soft tissues
• Dysphagia - difficulty swallowing
• Dysgeusia - foul, salty, or metallic taste sensation persistsin the mouth
• Weight loss


Common long-term side effects include:

• Rampant caries - rapidly growing dental decay
• Trismus - muscle spasms and limited mouth opening
• Xerostomia - dry mouth
• Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) - bone death after radiation therapy


About ORN

Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) is a severe, late complication of radiation therapy, commonly affecting the bones in the head and neck region, particularly the mandible (lower jaw). Dental extraction in the irradiated areas post radiotherapy (>50 Gy) remains the main risk factor for ORN. Post-radiation infections can also contribute to the development of ORN. People experience persistent pain, ranging from mild to severe in the affected area, and possible loss of substantial amount of bone tissue leading to pathological fractures in some cases. Management of ORN is difficult and can involve significant time and cost. Thorough dental examination and proper treatment of any dental issues before starting radiation therapy is crucial for the prevention of ORN.
