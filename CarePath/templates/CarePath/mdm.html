The Multidisciplinary Meeting (MDM)



The Multidisciplinary Meeting (MDM) is a special clinic where a team of dedicated health professionals from different areas come together to discuss and review cases involving head and neck issues. This team includes nurses, doctors, dentists, and other support staff who all collaborate to provide the best care possible. While many patients at the MDM have cancer, the clinic also sees individuals with benign (non-cancerous) conditions. The goal of your visit to the MDM is for this team to carefully review your situation and explore the best treatment options for you. After the meeting, you’ll have a one-on-one discussion with one of the doctors to talk through the options and next steps in your care. We're here to support you and help guide you through this process with compassion and understanding.


Getting ready for this appointment

Your appointment may be up to five hours long. Please arrange to be there from 8:00am until the early afternoon. Eat and drink as normal prior to the appointment (you do not need to fast). During this time different health professionals will come and meet with you. There are many patients that the MDM team will review and discuss; this could take time. Afterwards, a doctor will meet with you and your whānau/support person to talk about your treatment options. Feel free to bring along your favourite book, magazine or puzzle to keep yourself busy during the waiting times. Free Wi-Fi is also available.


Your clinicians

Your treatment and care will involve a number of healthcare professionals who can support you in different ways. You may meet some of them on the day. Below are some of the people you may meet and what they do.

1. Clinical Nurse Specialists (CNS) - Nurses with special knowledge of the head and neck. They will be your key contact person during your treatment.
2. Head and Neck Surgeons - Doctors who perform surgery to treat head and neck problems.
3. Speech Language Therapists - Health professionals who can help you with talking and swallowing.
4. Reconstructive Surgeons - Doctors who perform surgery to improve structure and function of areas damaged by cancer.
5. Dietitians - A health professional that can help you with eating and drinking.
6. Radiation Oncologists - Doctors trained in the use of radiation to treat cancer.
7. Medical Oncologists - Doctors trained in the use of medicines to treat cancer.
8. Dentist/Oral Medicine Specialists - Doctors who support you to look after your teeth and mouth.
9. Palliative Care Specialists - Doctors and nurses who can help you with the management of symptoms, such as pain.


What happens at the MDM

On arrival to the Outpatient Clinic you and your whānau/support person will be directed to a clinic room. The clinic room will be your base for the day. Most of your time will be spent in this room. During the morning members of the MDM team will come and meet with you privately. If the MDM is very busy you may be asked to wait in the main waiting room.


Clinical Examination

Part of the MDM process is to do a physical examination of you. This is done so the team has up to date information to base a decision on. The examination will take place in a private room. Two doctors and Clinical Nurse Specialists (CNS), including your local CNS, will be present. Whānau and support people are welcomed and encouraged to be with you. The examination takes about 10 minutes. The examination will include a video recording of the affected area of your head or neck by inserting a camera scope (a long, thin bendy tube with a camera attached) into your nose and/ or throat. You will get a numbing spray before the camera is inserted. All of this will be explained to you in more detail at the time. You will be able to ask questions. The video and audio are linked from the examination room to another room where the wider MDM team is. They are able to watch the examination and may ask questions through a microphone. After the MDM team has had a discussion about your case, a doctor will come and discuss your treatment options with you. They will also give you some written information. There may be a long wait for you to have this discussion with the doctor as there are many patients for the MDM team to review and discuss. Thank you for your patience during this time.


Support person

Most patients find it helpful to bring a whānau (family) member, a friend or other support person. Detailed information about your condition will be shared with you, including information about your treatment. It can be difficult to remember all the information given. A support person can help you understand the information and write things down. They can make sure your questions are answered.


Medications

Please bring a list of any medications you are taking. If you require any medications during the day, please bring them with you.


Food

A lunch box (usually containing a sandwich, fruit and snack) is provided for you as the person attending the MDM. If you have special dietary requirements, please bring your own food with you. A lunch box is not provided for the support people so please bring food for them.


Interpreter

If you or your whānau need help understanding English on the day of your MDM appointment, please let your clinical nurse specialist know.
